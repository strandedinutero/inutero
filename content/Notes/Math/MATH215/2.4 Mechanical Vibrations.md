# Mechanical Vibrations

Mechanical vibrations are really just applications of second order linear constant coefficient linear equations.

For example, suppose we have some mass $m$ on a string, its force over time can be modeled as:
$$
f(t) = mx'' + cx' + kx
$$
Where it is considered as forced if $f \neq 0$, free if $f=0$, damped if $c>0$ or undamped if $c=0$

Free undamped motion is just modeled by:

$$
mx'' + kx = 0
$$
Where the general solution is of the form:

$$
x(t) = A\cos(\omega_{0}t) +B\sin(\omega_{0}t)
$$
Where $\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$ 

Now, free undamped motion is just modeled by the homogeneous equation:
$$
mx'' + cx' + kx = 0
$$
Where we write:

$$
x'' + 2px'+\omega_{0}^2x = 0
$$
and $p = \frac{c}{2m}$.

To which we can solve using the same method in [[2.2 Solving Constant Coefficient 2nd Order ODEs]]. Note, when $c^2 - 4km > 0$ the system is overdamped and there are two distinct real roots. When $c^2 -4km = 0$ the system is critically damped and there is one real root of multiplicity 2 and it is $-p$. Whereas if $c^2 -4km < 0$ the system is underdamped and there are complex roots.

### 1. Natural Frequency

For the undamped system,

$$
\omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}
$$

this is usually called the **natural frequency** or **undamped natural frequency**.

### 2. Damped Natural Frequency

In the underdamped case, the oscillation frequency is not $\omega_0$, but

$$
\omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - p^2}.
$$

This is usually called the **damped natural frequency**.

### 3. Mass-Spring-Damper System

The model

$$
mx'' + cx' + kx = F(t)
$$

is usually called a **mass-spring-damper system**.

### 4. External Forcing / Driving Force

Instead of just saying “forced if $f \neq 0$,” people often say:

- **forced motion** or **driven motion** if $F(t) \neq 0$
- **free motion** if $F(t) = 0$

It is better to use $F(t)$ instead of $f(t)$, since $f$ is easy to confuse with other functions.

### Free Undamped Motion

$$
mx'' + kx = 0
$$

$$
x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t) + B\sin(\omega_0 t)
$$

### Free Damped Motion

$$
mx'' + cx' + kx = 0
$$

or equivalently

$$
x'' + 2px' + \omega_0^2 x = 0
$$

Then:

### Overdamped: $c^2 - 4mk > 0$

There are two distinct real roots, so

$$
x(t) = C_1 e^{r_1 t} + C_2 e^{r_2 t}
$$

There is **no oscillation**.

### Critically Damped: $c^2 - 4mk = 0$

There is one repeated root $r = -p$, so

$$
x(t) = (C_1 + C_2 t)e^{-pt}
$$

There is **no oscillation**.

### Underdamped: $c^2 - 4mk < 0$

There are complex roots

$$
r = -p \pm i\omega_d
$$

so

$$
x(t) = e^{-pt}\left(C_1\cos(\omega_d t) + C_2\sin(\omega_d t)\right)
$$

This is **oscillatory motion with exponentially decaying amplitude**.

- In the **undamped** case, amplitude stays constant.
- In the **damped** case, energy is lost over time.
- In the **underdamped** case, motion still oscillates.
- In the **overdamped** and **critically damped** cases, motion does not oscillate.

## Forced Vibrations

For a forced system of the form,

$$
mx'' + cx' + kx = F(t),
$$

the solution is

$$
x(t) = x_h(t) + x_p(t)
$$

where:

- $x_h$ is the **transient solution**
- $x_p$ is the **steady-state solution** or **particular solution**

If the forcing is sinusoidal, that also leads into **resonance**.

## The Friction Constant C

$$
c = 2m\sqrt{ \omega_{o}^2-\omega_{d}^22 }
$$

Where $\omega_{0}$ is the natural frequency and $\omega_{d}$ is the damped frequency.
