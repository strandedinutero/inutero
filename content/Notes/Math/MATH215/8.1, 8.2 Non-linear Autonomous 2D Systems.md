# Nonlinear Autonomous Planar Systems  
  
We study autonomous systems of the form  
$$  
\begin{cases}  
x' = F(x,y) \\  
y' = G(x,y)  
\end{cases}  
$$  
  
A **phase plane** is the $xy$-plane together with arrows/trajectories showing how solutions move.  
  
---  
  
## Nullclines  
  
The **nullclines** are the curves where one of the derivatives is zero.  
  
- The **$x$-nullcline** is found by solving  
$$  
F(x,y)=0  
$$  
Along this curve, $x'=0$, so trajectories have no horizontal motion.  
  
- The **$y$-nullcline** is found by solving  
$$  
G(x,y)=0  
$$  
Along this curve, $y'=0$, so trajectories have no vertical motion.  
  
### Why nullclines matter  
They help us determine the direction field:  
  
- If $x' = F(x,y) > 0$, arrows point to the right.  
- If $x' = F(x,y) < 0$, arrows point to the left.  
- If $y' = G(x,y) > 0$, arrows point upward.  
- If $y' = G(x,y) < 0$, arrows point downward.  
  
So by checking signs in each region separated by the nullclines, we can sketch the local flow.  
  
---  
  
## Equilibria / Critical Points  
  
An **equilibrium point** (or **critical point**) $(x^*,y^*)$ is a point where both derivatives are zero:  
$$  
F(x^*,y^*) = 0, \qquad G(x^*,y^*) = 0  
$$  
  
So equilibria occur where the nullclines intersect.  
  
At an equilibrium point,  
$$  
x'(t)=0, \qquad y'(t)=0  
$$  
so a solution starting there stays there forever.  
  
---  
  
## Jacobian Matrix  
  
To study the system near an equilibrium, form the **Jacobian**:  
$$  
J(x,y) =  
\begin{bmatrix}  
F_x(x,y) & F_y(x,y) \\  
G_x(x,y) & G_y(x,y)  
\end{bmatrix}  
$$  
  
At an equilibrium $(x^*,y^*)$, evaluate:  
$$  
A = J(x^*,y^*)  
$$  
  
This matrix gives the best linear approximation to the system near that equilibrium.  
  
---  
  
## Linearization Near $(x^*,y^*)$  
  
Let  
$$  
x = x^* + u, \qquad y = y^* + v  
$$  
where $u$ and $v$ are small perturbations.  
  
Then near $(x^*,y^*)$, the nonlinear system is approximated by the linear system  
$$  
\begin{bmatrix}  
u' \\  
v'  
\end{bmatrix}  
=  
J(x^*,y^*)  
\begin{bmatrix}  
u \\  
v  
\end{bmatrix}  
$$  
  
So the local behavior near the equilibrium is approximated by the phase portrait of the linear system  
$$  
\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}  
$$  
  
This is called the **linearization** of the system at $(x^*,y^*)$.  
  
---  
  
## Classification from Eigenvalues  
  
To classify an isolated equilibrium, compute the eigenvalues of  
$$  
A = J(x^*,y^*)  
$$  
  
Let the eigenvalues be $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$. Use [[3.5 2D Systems and Vector Fields]]
  
### 1. Saddle Point  
If the eigenvalues are real and have opposite signs:  
$$  
\lambda_1 < 0 < \lambda_2  
$$  
then the equilibrium is a **saddle**.  
  
- always **unstable**  
  
---  
  
### 2. Node  
If the eigenvalues are real and have the same sign:  
  
- If both are negative, the equilibrium is a **stable node** or **sink**  
- If both are positive, the equilibrium is an **unstable node** or **source**  
  
---  
  
### 3. Spiral / Focus  
If the eigenvalues are complex:  
$$  
\lambda = \alpha \pm i\beta, \qquad \beta \neq 0  
$$  
  
- If $\alpha < 0$, we get a **stable spiral**  
- If $\alpha > 0$, we get an **unstable spiral**  
  
---  
  
### 4. Center  
If the eigenvalues are purely imaginary:  
$$  
\lambda = \pm i\beta  
$$  
then the linearized system has a **center**.  
  
For the **nonlinear** system, this case is often **inconclusive**.  
  
---  
  
## Stability  
  
Let $(x^*,y^*)$ be an equilibrium.  
  
### Stable  
The equilibrium is **stable** if solutions starting sufficiently close stay close for all future time.  
  
### Unstable  
The equilibrium is **unstable** if there are solutions starting arbitrarily close that move away.  
  
### Asymptotically Stable  
The equilibrium is **asymptotically stable** if it is stable and, in addition, solutions starting close enough satisfy  
$$  
(x(t),y(t)) \to (x^*,y^*)  
\quad \text{as } t \to \infty  
$$  
  
### Quick eigenvalue rule  
If all eigenvalues of $J(x^*,y^*)$ have negative real part, the equilibrium is asymptotically stable.  
  
If at least one eigenvalue has positive real part, the equilibrium is unstable.  
  
---  
  
## When Linearization Is Inconclusive  
  
Linearization works well when the equilibrium is **hyperbolic**, meaning no eigenvalue has real part zero.  
  
It is **inconclusive** when at least one eigenvalue has real part zero, for example:  
  
- $\lambda = \pm i\beta$ purely imaginary  
- $\lambda = 0$  
- repeated zero-real-part cases  
  
In these situations, the nonlinear terms that were dropped may change the behavior completely.  
  
For example, the linearized system may look like a center, but the nonlinear system might actually be:  
- a stable spiral  
- an unstable spiral  
- something more complicated  
  
So in nonhyperbolic cases, we usually need extra phase-plane analysis, nullcline analysis, or other tools.  
  
---  
  
## Limit Cycles  
  
A **limit cycle** is an isolated closed trajectory in the phase plane.  
  
- **closed trajectory** means the motion is periodic  
- **isolated** means nearby trajectories are not also closed curves  
  
### Types  
- **stable limit cycle**: nearby trajectories spiral toward it  
- **unstable limit cycle**: nearby trajectories spiral away from it  
  
### Why it matters  
Linear systems can have centers with infinitely many closed orbits, but nonlinear systems can have a single isolated periodic orbit, which is a limit cycle.  
  
So a limit cycle is a genuinely nonlinear phenomenon.

## Trace-Determinant Test for a $2 \times 2$ Jacobian  
  
If  
$$  
A =  
\begin{bmatrix}  
a & b \\  
c & d  
\end{bmatrix}  
$$  
then define  
$$  
\tau = \operatorname{tr}(A)=a+d, \qquad \Delta = \det(A)=ad-bc  
$$  
  
The characteristic equation is  
$$  
\lambda^2 - \tau \lambda + \Delta = 0  
$$  
  
Let  
$$  
D = \tau^2 - 4\Delta  
$$  
be the discriminant.  
  
### Classification using $\tau,\Delta,D$  
  
- If $\Delta < 0$: **saddle**  
- If $\Delta > 0$ and $D > 0$ and $\tau < 0$: **stable node**  
- If $\Delta > 0$ and $D > 0$ and $\tau > 0$: **unstable node**  
- If $\Delta > 0$ and $D < 0$ and $\tau < 0$: **stable spiral**  
- If $\Delta > 0$ and $D < 0$ and $\tau > 0$: **unstable spiral**  
- If $\Delta > 0$ and $\tau = 0$ and $D < 0$: linearized **center**  
- If $\Delta = 0$ or $D = 0$: special / repeated eigenvalue case, check more carefully

## Standard Workflow for Phase Plane Problems  
  
Given  
$$  
x' = F(x,y), \qquad y' = G(x,y)  
$$  
  
### Step 1  
Find the nullclines:  
$$  
F(x,y)=0, \qquad G(x,y)=0  
$$  
  
### Step 2  
Find the equilibria by solving both equations simultaneously.  
  
### Step 3  
Use sign analysis on $F$ and $G$ to determine arrow directions in each region.  
  
### Step 4  
Compute the Jacobian:  
$$  
J(x,y)=  
\begin{bmatrix}  
F_x & F_y \\  
G_x & G_y  
\end{bmatrix}  
$$  
  
### Step 5  
Evaluate the Jacobian at each equilibrium:  
$$  
A = J(x^*,y^*)  
$$  
  
### Step 6  
Find eigenvalues of $A$ and classify the equilibrium.  
  
### Step 7  
Combine the local linear picture with the nullclines and direction field to sketch the phase portrait.  
  
### Step 8  
If linearization is inconclusive, rely more heavily on nullclines, invariant regions, conserved quantities, or additional theory.  
  
---  
  
## What to Remember Most  
  
The main idea is:  
$$  
\text{nullclines} \to \text{equilibria} \to \text{Jacobian} \to \text{eigenvalues} \to \text{local phase portrait}  
$$
