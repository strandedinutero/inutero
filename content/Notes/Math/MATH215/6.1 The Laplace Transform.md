The *Laplace Transform* is an incredibly efficient method that can be used to solve ODEs, in brief, the transform turns a differential equation into an algebraic one, and if the algebraic form can be solved, then applying the *inverse laplace transform* gives us the desired solution to the original ODE. 

The Laplace Transform essentially takes a function of time $(t\geq 0)$ and maps it to a function of frequency such that:
$$
\mathcal{L}\{f(t)\} := \int_{0}^\infty e^{ -st }f(t)dt = F(s)
$$
For example,

$$
f(t)=1 \implies {\mathcal{L}} \{f(t)\} = \int_{0}^\infty e^{ -st }dt=\frac{e^{ -st }}{-s} \bigg|_{t=0}^\infty = \lim_{ t \to \infty } \bigg (  \frac{e^{ -st }}{-s }- \frac{1}{-s} \bigg) = \frac{1}{s}
$$

## When Does a Laplace Transform Exist?

A Laplace Transform for a function $f(t)$ exists when $f(t)$ is of *exponential order* that is:
$$
|f(t)| \le Me^{ ct } \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \lim_{ t \to \infty } \frac{f(t)}{e^{ ct }}=0
$$

In essence, as long as $f(t)$ is continuous on the interval $[0, \infty)$ and of exponential order for some constant $c$ then $F(s)$ is defined for all $s>c$.

Note, for all functions of exponential order, their transforms decay at $\infty$.

## Uniqueness

If two functions are both continuous and of exponential order, and there exists some constant $c$ such that their Laplace Transforms are equal for all $s>c$ then the two functions are equal for all $t\geq 0$. 

## The Inverse Transform

Given a function $F(s)$ we can use the table above to get $f(t)$ such that:
$$
\mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F(s)\} := f(t)
$$

## The Shifting Property

$$
\mathcal{L}\{e^{ -at }f(t)\} = F(s+a)
$$
For example:
$$
F(s)= \frac{1}{(s^2+4s+8)}=\frac{1}{(s^2+2)+2^2}
$$
We know that:
$$
\mathcal{L}\{\sin(at)\}=\frac{a}{(s^2+a^2)}\quad \text{for} \quad a = 2, \quad \mathcal{L}\{\sin(2t)\}=\frac{2}{(s^2+4)}
$$
So, 
$$
\mathcal{L}\left\{ \frac{1}{2}\sin(2t) \right\} = \frac{1}{s^2+4}=F(s)
$$
Now substitute $F(s+2)$ and apply shifting:
$$
f(t) = \frac{1}{2}e^{ -2t }\sin(2t)
$$
