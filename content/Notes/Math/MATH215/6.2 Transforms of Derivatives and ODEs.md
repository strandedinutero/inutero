# Laplace Transforms of ODEs

Using [[Integration By Parts]] we can derive the following Laplace transforms from [[6.1 The Laplace Transform]] to obtain results for first, second, and third order derivatives:
$$
\begin{align}

& f(t) = F(s)  \\
& x'(t) = sX(s)-x(0) \\
& x''(t) = s^2X(s)  -sx(0) - x'(0) \\
& x'''(t) = s^3X(s) -s^2x(0)-sx'(0)-x''(0) \\

\end{align}
$$
In essence, the Laplace transform turns differentiation into multiplication by $s$. The ==goal== of this strategy is to isolate and solve for $X(s)$ so we can take the inverse Laplace transform and find a solution.

## Transfer Functions

A transfer function is a way to describe a linear system's response to an input. Basically it allows us to find the output for any input.

Essentially ==a transfer function is the ratio of the Laplace transform of the output to the Laplace transform of the input==, such that for some algebraic equation in $s$, $A(s)$:
$$
A(s)X(s) = F(s)
$$
The transfer function $H(s)$ is given by:

$$
H(s) = \frac{1}{A(s)}=\frac{X(s)}{F(s)}
$$


## Transforms of Integrals

Fortunately, the Laplace transform also allows us to deal with equations containing integrals. Such that:

$$
\mathcal{L}\left\{ \int_{0}^t f(\tau)d\tau \right\} = \frac{1}{s}F(s)
$$
and,

$$
\int_{0}^tf(\tau)d\tau=\mathcal{L^{-1}}\{\frac{1}{s}F(s)\}
$$

For example, take the equation:

$$
x(t)-t=\int_{0}^tx(\tau)d\tau 
$$
We can apply a transform to the LHS and use the theorem above for the RHS to obtain:
$$
X(s)-\frac{1}{s^2}=\frac{1}{s}X(s)
$$
Solving for $X(s)$ we get:
$$
X(s)=\frac{1}{s-1}-\frac{1}{s}
$$
Then the inverse transform is simply:
$$
\mathcal{L}^{-1}\left\{ \frac{1}{s-1} \right\}+\mathcal{L}^{-1}\{\frac{1}{s}\}= e^{ t }-1
$$

Which is a solution.
