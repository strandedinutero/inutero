# Exact Equations

Exact equations are equations that come up often in physics and engineering, these equations appear whenever there is some conservation law at play. Now, consider an equation $F(x, y)$ this is called the potential function.

For example let $F(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$, now suppose $F(x, y)$ is some constant $C$, we can take the total derivative of $F$:

$$
dF = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y}dy
$$
Which yields:
$$
dF= 2xdx + 2ydy
$$
Now, because $F(x, y) = 0$ we have that $dF = 0$ and our equation has the form:

$$
Mdx + Ndy = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad M + N\frac{dy}{dx}= 0
$$
Where $M = F_x = 2x$ and $N = F_y =2y$.

So how do we find the general solution to this example?

We know that $M=F_x = 2x$, so integrating this we see that $F = x^2 + A(y)$ for some function $A(y)$, which represents the constant of integration in this case. Now, differentiate this $F$ in $y$ and set it equal to $N$, then we can solve for $A(y)$ by integrating:

$$
\begin{align}
F & = x^2 + A(y)  \\
F_{y} & = x^2 \frac{dy}{dx} +A'(y) \\
2y  & = 0 + A'(y)  \\
A(y) & = y^2

\end{align}
$$
So we see indeed that our solution is $F(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$

## Poincare Lemma

If $M=F_{x}$ and $N+F_{y}$ are continuously differentiable functions of $(x, y)$ and:

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} =\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} \iff M_{y} = N_{x}
$$
(Basically if $F_{xy} = F_{yx}$) Then a potential function $F(x,y)$ exists.

### Note

Sometimes we find equations that of the form:

$$
Mdx + Ndy = 0
$$
that are very close to being exact but they are not quite exact, to fix this we can use an integrating factor $\mu(x,y)$ such that:
$$
\mu Mdx = \mu Ndy
$$

Where any solutions to this equation are also solutions to the the initial equation.

Now, for:
$$
\mu M_{y} + \mu N_{x} = 0
$$
to be exact, it needs to satisfy the Poincare Lemma such that:

$$
\mu M_{y} = \mu N_{x} \iff (M_{y}-N_{x})\mu = u_{x}N+u_{y}M
$$
Namely, we want:
$$
u' - \frac{M_{y}-N_{x}}{N}\mu = 0
$$
Note this looks eerily similar to a first order ODE seen in [[1.4 The Integrating Factor]]. except we have that:

$$
p(x) = \frac{M_{y}-N_{x}}{N}
$$
So, our integrating factor is simply:

$$
\mu(x) = e^{ \int \frac{M_{y}-N_{x}}{N}dx}
$$
or if we wanted it as a function of $y$ it would then become:

$$
\mu(y) = e^{ -\int\frac{M_{y}-N_{x}}{M}dy }
$$
