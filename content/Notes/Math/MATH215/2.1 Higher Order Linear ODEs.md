#math #math215 #ODEs


A second order (highest we will do in this class) linear ODE has the form:
$$
y'' + p(x)y'+q(x)y=f(x)
$$
Where if $f(x)= 0$ it is considered a homogeneous equation.

Now, suppose we have two solutions of a *linear* homogeneous equation, we then know we actually have many many more due to the *superposition theorem*. In essence, suppose $y_1$ and $y_2$ are both solutions to our equation. By linearity and the superposition theorem we then have that:

$$
y(x) = C_{1}y_{1}(x) + C_{2}y_{2}(x)
$$
Is another solution where $y_1$ and $y_2$ are ==linearly independent== (not constant multiples of each other).

This is called the *general solution*

## Existence and Uniqueness

if $p, q, f$ are all continuous functions on some interval then the homogeneous equation where $f(x)=0$ has exactly one solution that satisfies some initial conditions $y'(a) = b_1$ and $y(a) = b_0$

## Reduction of Order Method

Using the reduction of order method, if you have one solution that is non zero to a second order ODE, then you can find another one and compose the general solution out of those.

Basically, suppose you have one solution $y_1$ to the second order equation: $y'' + py' +qy =0$, then $y_2$ has the form:
$$
y_{2}(x) = y_{1}(x) \int \frac{e^{ -\int p(x)dx }}{y_{1}(x)}dx
$$
