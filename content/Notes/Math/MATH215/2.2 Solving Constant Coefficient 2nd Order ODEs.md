# Constant Coefficient 2nd Order ODEs

We know that $\frac{d}{dx}e^kx=ke^x$, so in general when solving constant coefficient second order ODEs we can take the following approach:

Suppose we have an equation of the form:
$$
ay'' + by' +cy = 0
$$
Try the solution $y = e^{rx}$ to obtain:

$$
ar^2e^{rx} + bre^{rx} + ce^{rx} = 0
$$
Dividing by $e^{rx}$ we get:
$$
ar^2 + br + c = 0
$$
Which is simply a quadratic which can be factored to solve for $r$ using:
$$
r = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{ b^2-4ac }}{2a}
$$

Then, if $r_1, r_2$ are real and distinct the general solution is simply:
$$
y = C_{1}e^{r_{1}x} + C_{2}e^{r_{2}x}
$$
Where if $r_1 = r_2$ then:

$$
y = (C_{1}+C_{2}x)e^{r_{1}x}
$$
## What If $b^2 - 4ac < 0$ ?

If $r_1, r_2$ are complex then we can use Euler's formula and complex numbers to write our solution.

## Euler's Formula

$$
\begin{align}
e^{i\theta} & = \cos \theta+i\sin \theta \\
e^{ -i\theta } & = \cos \theta-i\sin \theta
\end{align}
$$
Namely, for some complex root $r = a + ib$ we can write its exponential as:

$$
\begin{align}
e^{ a+ib } & = e^a(\cos b+i\sin b) \\
e^{ a+ib } & = e^{ a }\cos b+ie^{ a }\sin b
\end{align}
$$
Note, we use the convention $Re(a + ib) = a$ to denote the real component of the root, and $Im(a+ib) = ib$ to denote the imaginary part.

So, suppose we are dealing with complex roots and $r_1 = a + ib$, $r_2 = a - ib$ (its complement). Then, the general solution is as follows:

$$
\begin{align}
y = & C_{1}e^{ (a+ib)x } + C_{2}e^{ (a-ib)x } \\
y = & C_{1}e^{ ax }(\cos b+i\sin b) + C_{2}e^{ax}(\cos b-i\sin b)
\end{align}
$$
