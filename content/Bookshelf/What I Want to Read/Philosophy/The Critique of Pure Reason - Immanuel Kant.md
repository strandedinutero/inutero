Also referred to as Kant's &quot;first critique,&quot; it was followed in 1788 by the Critique of Practical Reason and in 1790 by the Critique of Judgment. In the preface to the first edition Kant explains what he means by a critique of pure reason: &quot;I do not mean by this a critique of books and systems, but of the faculty of reason in general, in respect of all knowledge after which it may strive independently of all experience.&quot; Before Kant, it was generally held that truths of reason must be analytic, meaning that what is stated in the predicate must already be present in the subject (for example, &quot;An intelligent man is intelligent&quot; or &quot;An intelligent man is a man&quot;). In either case, the judgment is analytic because it is ascertained by analyzing the subject. It was thought that all truths of reason, or necessary truths, are of this kind: that in all of them there is a predicate that is only part of the subject of which it is asserted. If this were so, attempting to deny anything that could be known a priori (for example, &quot;An intelligent man is not intelligent&quot; or &quot;An intelligent man is not a man&quot;) would involve a contradiction. It was therefore thought that the law of contradiction is sufficient to establish all a priori knowledge."

# The Critique of Pure Reason

*by Immanuel Kant*

The Critique of Pure Reason is a foundational philosophical work by Immanuel Kant, published in 1781, that explores the limits and scope of human reason, particularly in relation to metaphysics and epistemology. It aims to establish a critical framework for understanding how we gain knowledge and the nature of reality, distinguishing between what can be known through experience and what lies beyond it.
